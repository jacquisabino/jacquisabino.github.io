It should come as no shock that OpenAI has created an abundance of loopholes to collect personal data. Particularly, from third parties. But, I'm an optimist. OpenAI has divided the category of personal data collected into three subcategories. Personal Data the user provides. Personal Data OpenAI receives from a users' use of OpenAI services. Information OpenAI receives from other sources. The most interesting of which being the latter.

The first subcategory is pretty self-explanatory. This would be your account information, prompts to ChatGPT, messages sent in the OpenAI community forum, and data used for the personalization and enhanced experience of third-party apps such as Canva, Coursera, Adobe Photoshop, and Grammarly. This is actually my first time learning about 'ChatGPT apps'. They are basically multiple AI-assistants that give you seamless exepriences for a data trade. Communication Information is basic, when you communicate with OpenAI via email or social media, they can collect your name, phone number, email, anything needed to contact you, and contents of those messages. I actually find it pretty funny thinking of hundreds of Twitter and Instagram handles associated with "Your the devil, die", or "Why no porn?". Is there any use? Isolated, no. But I can definately see advantages to adding this little bit of character defining data to a user profile that likely cosists of personally identifying data such as IP address, hometown, language spoken, schools attended, preferred names, emails, etc... And once you've reached this stage, you can easily start mapping to other users, to identify friends and family. That part, however, is not so clear. Since up to this point OpenAI has only mentioned collecting user interactions via their Services provided. No proxies. Contact Data is just your device address book. When you create an account, your phone number is stored as a hashed version of that number onto OpenAI servers. If you choose to upload your contact list, your address book is used to iterate through those numbers, create hashed versions of them, check if those numbers exist on OpenAI servers, if they do, you receive a suggested follow, if not, this hashed number is stored as just that. It is some contact disassociated from the rest of the information you provide in your address book. But it is a connection to you. Later, if that contact chooses to create a ChatGPT account, their phone nuber will be hashed, checked amongst the current hashed numbers on OpenAI servers, and found amongst your contact list. Follow suggested to you since you chose to upload their contact data, with your contact info. If they did not choose to upload existing contacts that contain your number, they will only receive an option to follow you once you have followed them. Otherwise, they should not see a suggested follow. Original user -> hashed number -> friend's hashed number. Friend creates ChatGPT account. Original user -> Friend account. Friend Account -> hashed number. No connection is created from friend to you. Other information users provide would be information collected from OpenAI events, or from vendors operating on their behalf. I have yet to witness this but let's say OpenAI is tabling at your University, they have you fill out a survey asking you your, name, age, and how you feel about ChatGPT. Since this would be used to verify your identity, you'd have to have an OpenAI account to participate. Since this vendor could verify your identity as an existing OpenAI user, your response to the survey has been linked to your profile. You are additionally part of a submap of OpenAI accounts who filled out that same survey at that specific University.

Now onto Personal Data OpenAI receives from an account's use of Services. This is all the background information sent around the internet. When someone uses a service like ChatGPT, your browser and device allows information to be relayed to the server you are requesting from. You ask a question to ChatGPT -> your device carries that question, IP address, browser type, settings, date, time, and how you interact with their Services -> you receive a tailored response. It should be noted that whether or not you have an account when you message the ChatGPT AI chatbot, you agree to terms and to having read the privacy policy. Let's imagine a scenario when this information is given to OpenAI. Say it is 10pm, February 11th 2026 and you realized you have an assignemnt due at midnight. Now, you absolutely despise ChatGPT because of the waste of energy, water, general resources, and big tech is just a huge ick. But when it comes to homework, you have to do what you have to do. So, you navigate to ChatGPT.com and start a new chat with all your old conversations lingering off to the left. Their one line summaries remind you of previous bouts of desperation. "Resume template for entry level IT roles", "Fix Grammar errors on this paper", "Difference between x86 and ARM", "Binary Bomb lab solution". So here you are, asking it to generate examples of pointers in C, what it means to dereference, what a memory leak is, etc... At this point, your device identifier is given, the fact that you are using the most recent version of Google Chrome (you like to keep up-to-date with your personal security so regular updates are a must) is given, Februrary 11th 2026 10pm CST is given, and the fact that you're using the chatbot is given. This falls under log information, which is generally most useful when something goes wrong. The purpose of a log is to keep track of just enough information to diagnose but not so much that you overwhelm your team with useless data and loose time in the process. Businesses will keep various versions of them them for auditing, monitoring performance, and debugging. For anything more specific, we move to OpenAI's collection of "usage data". To continue with the above example, at the end of your session the fact that you spent 45 minutes on chatgpt.com is given, and generalizable information is gathered and likely used in aggregate with your previous search topics to create a more robust profile on you. Such as, "C programming language", "Pointers", "Coding", "Job in Tech", and "Student". Perhaps you weren't satisfied with some responses so you clicked the thumbs down or thumbs up button. Maybe you even went out of your way to report something as misiniformation or disinformation. Do you use the group chat function? Or maybe your a fan of the forum? Those users you interacted with are now recorded. Honestly, I struggled figuring out how time zone, country, dates and time of access were useful. TimeZone: CST, America, Feb 11th 2026 11pm. Broadly speaking, it could be used to track patterns of access. My guess is a lot of Americans or more Industrialized countries and regions within those countries have more need for OpenAI Services. Maybe there's some patterns to be recognized about your usage of their chatbot. Or there's an overarching pattern about students' usage of the chatbot. Could this influence where they choose to host servers? Even though everything feels wireless, magical, and isntant, distance is still very real, hardware is still very real. And the closer a data center is to their users and maintenance teams, the better service you, but most importantly, their paying customers, recieve. Now the next time you navigate to ChatGPT.com, and start typing whatever it is you need, the suggested searches will appear more specific to your use of the chatbot. I would like to think that on OpenAI's side, there are different models assigned to programming heavy topics and can prepare for that as well without you needing to prompt it first. Perhaps OpenAI notices a spike in Math and Science heavy questions a couple hours before midnight on Friday, not just from your IP address but thousands. To prevent a crash and keep paying customers paying, their inference servers, those responsible for generating responses, would need to adapt to this influx of requests to remain as fast and reliable as your request made Monday at 2pm. Interestingly enough, we don't really see any downsides of these server-side optimization techniques, though they exist. What about lower-level information? Such as user agent and version, type of computer or mobile device, and your computer connection. A user agent is a string web browsers send to servers with details about browser and device. If you go to "WhatMyUserAgent.com" you can see for yourself what your computer id looks like. If you want to create a game to be hosted on a website, you want it to be consistent for all users, despite differences in Browser, Operating System, or the various version of both. Otherwise, you risk loosing customers, clients, or just people who really want to play that game. The same goes for chatbots. There is such thing as developing with intentional diregard. But enough of that, what if someone doesn't have your best interest at heart? Just like a developer can cater to your needs based on browser, OS, and device specificity. An adversary can cater to their needs based on the same infromation. If an exploit is discovered on the Google Chrome browser, a user agent is 1000% beenficial to anyone looking to profit. Location data benefits the company for reasons I stated above. Trends in usage, palcement of inference servers, load balancing in data centers, addressing hardware, software, and network security failures on OpenAI's side. What about writing styles and subtlties of city, state, or demographic culture? Finally, 'cookies and similar technologies'. We've all heard of cookies, little files stored on our device that allow web browsers to display data specific to us and our previous visit/s to that site. Most people have correlated them with evil data brokers, malicious hackers, and advertisers. All of those would be accurate, but we agree to them anyways, it's easier to. I don't like the added friction of re-entering passwords or 2fa as much as the next guy. But just like we create obstacles for our future selves to benefit from (like keeping indulgent snacks out of site or layering our alarms) we should be willing to do the same for our internet health. Much of the data described above can be stored and saved by cookies. Unsurprisingly, similar technologies have arisen due to massive crackdown on the cookie systems. Most of these alternatives serve the same purpose. Web beacons and referral urls track your activity across sites. Device/broswer fingerprinting and AD ID's are capable of identifying you even with a VPN.
